\section{Object detection}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\Large\color{popblue}\textbf{Object detection}}\\[0.9em]
    \begin{minipage}{0.82\textwidth}\centering\small
      What \emph{and} where - a class and a box for every object in the frame.
    \end{minipage}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{From "what" to "what and where"}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.44\textwidth}
    For \emph{one} object, no new machinery is needed - just a \textbf{longer label
    vector}:
    \[
      \yv =
      \begin{bmatrix}
        b_x\\ b_y\\ b_h\\ b_w\\ c_o\\ c_{\text{pom}}\\ c_{\text{apple}}\\ c_{\text{quince}}
      \end{bmatrix}
      \begin{matrix}
        \text{\footnotesize box center }x\\
        \text{\footnotesize box center }y\\
        \text{\footnotesize box height}\\
        \text{\footnotesize box width}\\
        \text{\footnotesize object present? (binary)}\\
        \text{\footnotesize class, one-hot}\\
        \text{\footnotesize class, one-hot}\\
        \text{\footnotesize class, one-hot}
      \end{matrix}
    \]
    \column{0.56\textwidth}
    Classification \textbf{and} box regression, one network: the box coordinates are
    just four regression outputs ([01]'s loss next to L11's).
    \vskip 6pt
    \pause
    But the belt photo holds \textbf{ten} fruit. The next one, \textbf{three}.
    \vskip 4pt
    \fcolorbox{armred}{armred!8}{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\small
      A photo can contain \textbf{any number} of objects - but a network has a
      \textbf{fixed output size}. The whole field of object detection is engineering
      around that mismatch.}}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{IoU: the overlap score}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.44\textwidth}
    Detectors output many boxes, so we constantly ask: \textbf{how much do two boxes
    agree?}
    \[
      \text{IoU}(A, B) = \frac{\text{area}(A \cap B)}{\text{area}(A \cup B)}
    \]
    $1$ = identical boxes, $0$ = no overlap; in practice $\text{IoU} \ge 0.5$ usually
    counts as "the same object".
    \vskip 6pt
    \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\small
      Worked example: $A = (0,0)$-$(4,4)$, $B = (2,0)$-$(6,4)$. Intersection $= 8$,
      union $= 16 + 16 - 8 = 24$, so $\text{IoU} = 8/24 = \mathbf{1/3}$.}}
    \column{0.56\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{iou_only.pdf}
    \end{center}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{NMS: from a flood of boxes to a clean answer}
  A detector proposes \textbf{many overlapping candidates} per object.
  \textbf{Non-max suppression} cleans up. \textbf{(Click to advance.)}
  \vskip 1pt
  \begin{center}
    \only<1>{\includegraphics[width=0.50\linewidth]{nms_anim_0.pdf}}%
    \only<2>{\includegraphics[width=0.50\linewidth]{nms_anim_1.pdf}}%
    \only<3>{\includegraphics[width=0.50\linewidth]{nms_anim_2.pdf}}%
    \only<4>{\includegraphics[width=0.50\linewidth]{nms_anim_3.pdf}}%
    \only<5>{\includegraphics[width=0.50\linewidth]{nms_anim_4.pdf}}%
  \end{center}
  \vskip 1pt
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      Repeat until no boxes remain: \textbf{keep} the highest-scoring box, \textbf{drop}
      every neighbor whose IoU with it exceeds a threshold (here $0.45$). You will turn
      this knob yourself in HW4.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Scoring a detector: mAP in one line}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item A predicted box is \textbf{correct} if the class matches and its IoU with a
      true box passes a threshold (say $0.5$).
    \item That makes every prediction a TP or FP - so \textbf{precision} and
      \textbf{recall} are exactly L12's, computed over boxes.
    \item \textbf{AP} $=$ area under the precision-recall curve, per class;
      \textbf{mAP} $=$ the mean over classes. Benchmarks also sweep the IoU threshold
      ($0.5$ to $0.95$) and average once more.
  \end{itemize}
  \vskip 8pt
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      No new metric theory: mAP is the \textbf{L12 machinery wrapped around boxes},
      swept over IoU thresholds.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Two families of detectors}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Two-stage}: first \emph{propose} candidate regions, then classify and
    refine each.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item R-CNN (2014) $\to$ Fast $\to$ \textbf{Faster R-CNN} (2015)
      \item accurate, but two passes of work
    \end{itemize}
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{One-stage}: predict all boxes and classes in \emph{one} forward pass.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{YOLO} ("You Only Look Once", 2016), SSD (2016)
      \item real-time - video-rate detection
    \end{itemize}
  \end{columns}
  \vskip 8pt
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      Two-stage bought \textbf{accuracy}, one-stage bought \textbf{speed} - and a decade
      of iteration has mostly closed the gap.}}
  \vskip 6pt
  In practice, YOLO became the industry default: you \texttt{pip install} a pretrained
  one and fine-tune it on \emph{your} boxes (L18's recipe). That \textbf{is} the sorting
  line's counting camera.
  \vskip 4pt
  {\scriptsize\color{gray} Girshick et al. 2014; Ren et al. 2015; Redmon et al. 2016;
  Liu et al. 2016.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{How one-stage works: the YOLO grid}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.42\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Divide the image into an $S \times S$ grid ($7 \times 7$ in the paper).
      \item \textbf{Each cell} predicts $2$ candidate boxes - $(x, y, w, h,
        \text{confidence})$ - plus class probabilities.
      \item All $98$ boxes come out of \textbf{one forward pass}, as one $7 \times 7
        \times 30$ tensor. That is why it runs in real time.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\small
      A flood of overlapping candidates by \emph{design} - exactly why \textbf{NMS}
      exists.}}
    \column{0.58\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{yolo_grid.png}\\[2pt]
      {\scriptsize The paper's own diagram (Redmon et al., "You Only Look Once",
      2016).}
    \end{center}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Separable convolutions: the idea}
  Real-time detection on a \textbf{belt camera} (or a phone) has no data center behind
  it. The efficiency trick that makes it fit - used by MobileNet-class backbones -
  splits one convolution into two cheap ones:
  \vskip 6pt
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Depthwise}: one small filter \emph{per channel}, each filtering its
      own channel - no mixing across depth.
    \item \textbf{Pointwise}: a $1 \times 1$ convolution mixes the channels back
      together - L17's per-pixel dense layer, doing exactly its job.
  \end{enumerate}
  \vskip 8pt
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      Recall (L16): a \emph{standard} filter spans the \textbf{full input depth} - space
      and channels processed together. Separable says: do \textbf{space first, channels
      second}. Almost as expressive, far cheaper - as the next slide counts.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Separable convolutions: the count}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
    Input $12 \times 12 \times 3$, $5\times5$ filters, $256$ output channels
    $\Rightarrow$ output $8 \times 8$. Count multiplications:
    \vskip 4pt
    {\small
    \textbf{Standard:}
    \[
      256 \cdot \underbrace{(5 \cdot 5 \cdot 3)}_{\text{per stop}} \cdot
      \underbrace{(8 \cdot 8)}_{\text{stops}} = \mathbf{1{,}228{,}800}
    \]
    \textbf{Depthwise} $+$ \textbf{pointwise}:
    \[
      \underbrace{3 \cdot 25 \cdot 64}_{4{,}800} +
      \underbrace{256 \cdot 3 \cdot 64}_{49{,}152} = \mathbf{53{,}952}
    \]}
    \column{0.53\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \includegraphics[width=0.94\linewidth]{separable_count.pdf}
    \end{center}
  \end{columns}
  \vskip 2pt
  \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      $\sim$\textbf{23}$\times$ fewer multiplications for the same output shape. This is
      why a pretrained detector runs \emph{on} the sorting line, not in a cloud.}}
\end{frame}

\section{Segmentation}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\Large\color{popblue}\textbf{Segmentation}}\\[0.9em]
    \begin{minipage}{0.82\textwidth}\centering\small
      Every pixel gets an answer.
    \end{minipage}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Semantic vs instance}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.42\textwidth}
    Segmentation $=$ \textbf{per-pixel classification}.
    \vskip 4pt
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Semantic}: every pixel gets a class label - all our pomegranates
        merge into \emph{one} fruit mask (task-zoo panel 4).
      \item \textbf{Instance}: each fruit gets its \textbf{own} mask.
        \textbf{Mask R-CNN} $=$ Faster R-CNN $+$ a small mask head per region
        (He et al., 2017).
      \item \textbf{Panoptic}: both at once - every pixel labeled \emph{and} grouped
        into instances.
    \end{itemize}
    \column{0.58\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \includegraphics[width=0.93\linewidth]{maskrcnn_framework.png}\\[2pt]
      {\scriptsize Mask R-CNN: detection boxes plus a per-region mask branch
      (He et al., 2017).}
    \end{center}
    \vskip 3pt
    \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\small
      And from the L17 epilogue: \textbf{SAM} made segmentation a \emph{promptable}
      foundation-model capability - click an object, get its mask.}}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Transposed convolution: the output must be an image}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.52\textwidth}
    \small
    Convs and pools \textbf{shrink} maps - but a per-pixel answer needs \textbf{full
    resolution} back: a learnable way \emph{up}.
    \vskip 4pt
    A \textbf{transposed convolution} upsamples: each input cell \emph{stamps} the
    kernel onto the output, overlaps add.
    \vskip 4pt
    {\small Matrix view, one line: a conv is a sparse multiply $\yv = K\mathbf{z}$; the
    transposed conv uses $K^\top$'s \emph{sparsity pattern} to go back up - with its own
    learned weights. Needed whenever the \textbf{output is an image}.}
    \vskip 4pt
    \fcolorbox{armorange}{armorange!12}{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\small
      \textbf{Watch-out: checkerboard artifacts.} Uneven kernel overlaps tile the output
      with a checker pattern. Fixes: kernel size divisible by the stride, or
      upsample-then-convolve (Odena et al., 2016).}}
    \column{0.48\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \begin{tikzpicture}[font=\scriptsize]
        % input 2x2
        \foreach \i in {0,1}\foreach \j in {0,1}
          \node[draw, minimum size=0.52cm, fill=armblue!22] at (\j*0.56,-\i*0.56) {};
        \node[draw=armblue, very thick, minimum size=0.52cm] at (0,0) {};
        \node at (0.28,-1.5) {input $2\times2$};
        % arrow
        \draw[->, thick] (1.6,-0.28) -- (2.5,-0.28)
          node[midway, above, align=center] {transposed\\conv};
        % output 4x4 with stamped region
        \foreach \i in {0,1,2,3}\foreach \j in {0,1,2,3}
          \node[draw, minimum size=0.52cm, fill=paramgreen!10]
            at (3.1+\j*0.56,0.56-\i*0.56) {};
        \foreach \i in {0,1,2}\foreach \j in {0,1,2}
          \node[draw, minimum size=0.52cm, fill=armblue!35]
            at (3.1+\j*0.56,0.56-\i*0.56) {};
        \node at (3.94,-1.78) {output $4\times4$};
        \node[armblue, align=center] at (3.94,1.35)
          {one cell stamps a $3\times3$ kernel};
      \end{tikzpicture}
    \end{center}
    \vskip 4pt
    {\small Segmentation needs it now; \emph{generators} reuse it at the end of this
    lecture.}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Dilated convolution: context without losing resolution}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
    \small
    Insert \textbf{gaps} into the kernel: a $3\times3$ kernel with dilation $d$ taps
    cells $d$ apart. \textbf{(Click to widen.)}
    \vskip 4pt
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Same $9$ weights, wider view - no pooling, no lost pixels.
      \item L17 grew the receptive field by \emph{stacking}; dilation grows it
        \textbf{per layer}.
      \item Beyond images: \textbf{WaveNet} audio and TCN time-series models stack
        dilated 1D convs.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\small
      Segmentation's dilemma solved: see the \textbf{whole scene} while keeping
      \textbf{per-pixel} detail.}}
    \column{0.53\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \only<1>{\includegraphics[height=0.64\textheight]{dilation_anim_0.pdf}}%
      \only<2>{\includegraphics[height=0.64\textheight]{dilation_anim_1.pdf}}%
      \only<3>{\includegraphics[height=0.64\textheight]{dilation_anim_2.pdf}}%
    \end{center}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Stack dilations and the field explodes}
  \begin{center}
    \includegraphics[height=0.68\textheight]{receptive_field_dilated.pdf}
  \end{center}
  \vskip 2pt
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      Doubling the dilation each layer ($d = 1, 2, 4$) grows the receptive field
      \textbf{exponentially}: $15 \times 15$ instead of $7 \times 7$, for the same nine

\begin{frame}{U-Net: the segmentation workhorse}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.40\textwidth}
    \small
    Both tools on the table - the architecture writes itself:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Down}: convs $+$ pooling compress the image into context.
      \item \textbf{Up}: transposed convs climb back to full resolution.
      \item \textbf{Skips}: early, sharp maps are \emph{concatenated} across - detail
        the bottleneck destroyed.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 3pt
    \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\small
      L17's skips again - there they rescued \textbf{optimization}, here they carry
      \textbf{resolution}. Dilated convs often grow context mid-net.}}
    \column{0.60\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{unet_paper.png}\\[2pt]
      {\scriptsize The original U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) - medical imaging's
      favorite architecture.}
    \end{center}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

  The pomegranate line, end to end:
  \vskip 4pt

      \textbf{Question on the line} & \textbf{Task} & \textbf{Tool}\\

  on MRI slices, cracks on factory parts.
  \vskip 4pt
  \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      One backbone vocabulary - conv, pool, skip, upsample - and the \textbf{output
      structure} turns it into whichever tool the problem needs.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Same idea, other shapes: 1D and 3D}
  Nothing about convolution says "image". The window slides over whatever grid the data
  lives on:
  \vskip 6pt
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{1D} - sequences: accelerometer traces for activity recognition,
      audio, character-level text. L16 \emph{opened} with a 1D kernel sliding over a
      sequence - that was the real thing, not a warm-up.
    \item \textbf{2D} - images: this whole chapter.
    \item \textbf{3D} - volumes: video (stacked frames), MRI and CT scans - the kernel
      slides in $x$, $y$ \emph{and} $z$.
  \end{itemize}
  \vskip 8pt
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      Convolution is a \textbf{structural prior} - "nearby means related, patterns
      repeat" - not an image trick. Wherever that assumption holds, the same layer
      works.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The inductive-bias ledger}
  What this chapter really bought us - stated honestly:
  \vskip 6pt
  \begin{itemize}
    \item CNNs \textbf{assume} locality and translation equivariance ("nearby means
      related, a detector useful here is useful there").
    \item When the assumption is \emph{true} - images, audio, volumes - it acts as
      \textbf{regularization by architecture}: win with \emph{less data}.
    \item When it is \emph{false}, it costs you: \textbf{tabular data} still belongs to
      trees and boosting (ch4, L14's honest-scope frame), and images where
      \textbf{global context} dominates strain the local prior.
  \end{itemize}
  \vskip 8pt
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{
      There is no universally best architecture - only assumptions that \textbf{match or
      mismatch} the data. Choosing the bias \emph{is} the modeling decision.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Hand-off: Vision Transformers}
  The L17 epilogue's loose thread, tied:
  \vskip 6pt
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ViT} cuts the image into patches and feeds them to a transformer as a
      \emph{sequence} - \textbf{no convolution at all} (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021).
    \item It \textbf{drops the convolutional prior} - and buys it back with
      \textbf{scale}: with enough pretraining data, attention \emph{learns} locality
      instead of assuming it.
    \item Small data? The CNN prior still wins. Web-scale data? Attention takes the
      frontier. The ledger from two slides ago, live in 2026.
  \end{itemize}
  \vskip 8pt
  \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      Attention, transformers and the GenAI stack get their own chapters - vision handed
      the baton; we follow it there next.}}
\end{frame}

\section*{Recap}

\begin{frame}{Recap}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{output structure defines the task}: label $\to$ box $\to$ box per
      object $\to$ class per pixel.
    \item Detection vocabulary: \textbf{IoU} (our worked $1/3$), \textbf{NMS} (keep the
      best, drop overlapping neighbors), \textbf{mAP} ($=$ L12 over boxes).
    \item Two families: two-stage (Faster R-CNN) vs one-stage (\textbf{YOLO}: $7\times7$
      grid, all boxes in one pass).
    \item \textbf{Separable convs} make it fit on a belt camera ($\sim$23$\times$ fewer
      multiplications).
    \item Segmentation: semantic vs instance; \textbf{U-Net} $=$ down, up
      (\textbf{transposed convs}), skips - with \textbf{dilation} for cheap context.
    \item Convolution is a \textbf{prior with a domain}: 1D/2D/3D where locality holds,
      trees on tabular, attention at web scale.
  \end{itemize}

\end{frame}
