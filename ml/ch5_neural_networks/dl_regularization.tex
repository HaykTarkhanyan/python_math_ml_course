% --- OUR SPRINKLE (after weight decay): it's Ridge you already know ---
\begin{frame}{Weight decay is Ridge -- you've already met it}
  The penalty LMU just added, $R_{\text{reg}} = R_{\text{emp}} + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\lVert\bm\theta\rVert_2^2$,
  is \textbf{exactly Ridge regression} from the regression chapter -- now called \emph{weight
  decay} because the update
  $\;\bm\theta \leftarrow \bm\theta(1-\alpha\lambda) - \alpha\nabla R_{\text{emp}}\;$ shrinks
  every weight a little each step.
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item Smaller weights $\Rightarrow$ a smoother function $\Rightarrow$ less overfitting -- the
      same story as Ridge, now applied to a million weights.
    \item In PyTorch it is \textbf{one argument}: \texttt{Adam(..., weight\_decay=1e-4)}.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    The network is still ``a stack of regressions'', so the \emph{regressions'} regularizer
    still applies. Nothing here is NN-specific -- only the scale.}}
\end{frame}

% --- OUR SPRINKLE (after early stopping): read the training curves ---
\begin{frame}{Read your training curves}
  Early stopping needs a signal: plot \textbf{train and validation loss every epoch} -- the
  single most useful diagnostic in all of training.
  \vfill
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.54\textwidth}
    \begin{center}\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig/training_curves_4cases.pdf}\end{center}
    \column{0.46\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{Overfitting:} train keeps falling, validation turns \emph{up} -- stop at the
        valley (that \emph{is} early stopping).
      \item \textbf{Underfitting:} both stay high -- too much regularization or too few epochs.
      \item \textbf{Good:} both fall, validation a little above train.
    \end{itemize}
  \end{columns}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    Diagnose \emph{under- vs over-fitting from the curves first}, then reach for the matching
    knob -- don't turn all of them at once.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The regularization toolbox: which knob when}
  \begin{center}\small
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{3.3cm} p{7.5cm}@{}}
    \textbf{Technique} & \textbf{What it does / when to reach for it} \\ \hline
    Weight decay (L2) & shrink weights ($=$ Ridge); raise $\lambda$ if overfitting \\[3pt]
    Early stopping & stop at the validation valley; free, effectively always on \\[3pt]
    Dropout & train a crowd of subnetworks ($=$ bagging inside one net); rate $0.2$--$0.5$ \\[3pt]
    Data augmentation & grow the data with \emph{label-preserving} transforms \\[3pt]
    Good init $+$ BatchNorm & (last chunk) keep signal \& gradients healthy -- a mild regularizer too \\
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    All fight the same thing -- a high-capacity model \textbf{memorising} the training set.
    \textbf{Diagnose from the curves, then turn one knob.} Dropout is the NN-specific one; the
    rest you have met before (Ridge, bagging, cross-validation).}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Next: optimizing the training itself}
  We can now \textbf{build}, \textbf{train}, and \textbf{regularize} a network. The last piece
  is making training \emph{faster and more reliable} than plain SGD:
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{Momentum} -- roll through noise and small bumps instead of zig-zagging.
    \item \textbf{Adam} -- a per-parameter adaptive step size; the common default.
    \item \textbf{Learning-rate schedules} -- warmup, decay, cosine.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    That is the next chunk (LMU's \emph{optimization} week) -- the tricks that make SGD actually
    converge well on huge models.}}
\end{frame}
