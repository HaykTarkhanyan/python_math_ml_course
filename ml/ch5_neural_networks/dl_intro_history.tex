\begin{frame}{The state of the field (2026)}
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{Foundation models rule.} A handful of huge pretrained models, \emph{adapted}
      (prompting, fine-tuning, tools) to thousands of tasks -- not one model per task.
    \item \textbf{Multimodal by default.} One model handles text, images, audio, and video
      together.
    \item \textbf{Reasoning models.} Models now ``think'' longer at inference
      (\emph{test-time compute}) -- big gains on math and code.
    \item \textbf{Open weights caught up.} \textbf{DeepSeek-R1} (Jan 2025) matched top closed
      models on math/code, reportedly trained for $\sim$\$6M, weights released.
    \item \textbf{Agents.} Models don't just answer -- they browse, run code, and call tools.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    \textbf{Two 2024 Nobel Prizes} went to neural-network work -- \textbf{Physics} (Hopfield \&
    Hinton) and \textbf{Chemistry} (AlphaFold). The field the intro called ``not new'' is now
    mainstream science.}}
\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{The state of AI today: the industry}
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{NVIDIA sells the shovels.} Almost every model trains on its GPUs; it holds
      $\sim$80\% of the AI-chip market and in \textbf{July 2025 became the first company ever
      worth \$4\,trillion} (past Apple \& Microsoft). Its Blackwell chips are sold out.
    \item \textbf{Open weights caught up.} Open models (\textbf{DeepSeek}, \textbf{Qwen},
      \textbf{Kimi}, \textbf{Llama}, \textbf{Mistral}) now trail the best closed models by only
      \emph{months}, at a fraction of the price -- four of the top five come from Chinese labs.
    \item \textbf{The next frontier is ``physical AI''.} NVIDIA's Jensen Huang: ``every
      industrial company will become a robotics company'' -- pitching humanoid robots as a
      $\sim$\$40\,trillion market and selling the stack (world models) to train them.
    \item \textbf{Frontier models keep leaping.} Anthropic's \textbf{Claude Fable 5} (2026) can
      work \emph{for days} in an agent harness -- planning, delegating to sub-agents, and
      checking its own work.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armorange}{armorange!12}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    \textbf{The gold-rush pattern:} so far the surest profits go to whoever sells the
    ``picks and shovels'' (the compute), more than to the model builders themselves.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Case study: an AI rewrote Bun in 11 days}
  \textbf{Bun} is a fast JavaScript runtime, written in \textbf{Zig}. In July 2026 its creator
  used a \emph{pre-release} \textbf{Claude Fable 5} to rewrite the whole thing in \textbf{Rust}:
  \vskip 4pt
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item $\sim$\textbf{50 Claude Code agents} in parallel, peaking at $\sim$\textbf{1{,}300
      lines of code per minute} -- over \textbf{1 million lines} of Rust in \textbf{11 days}.
    \item Cost $\sim$\textbf{\$165{,}000} in API usage.
    \item The result passed \textbf{100\% of Bun's 1M+ test assertions} on every supported
      platform.
    \item \emph{Why Rust:} mixing Zig's manual memory with JavaScript's garbage collector kept
      producing use-after-free and memory-leak bugs.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armorange}{armorange!12}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    \textbf{Reality check.} Not everyone is convinced -- Zig's creator called the output
    ``unreviewed slop''. But a task that would take a team \emph{months} ran, and passed its
    tests, in under two weeks.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Everyday AI: language assistants}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.73\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item Chat assistants -- \textbf{ChatGPT}, \textbf{Claude}, \textbf{Gemini} -- are the
        fastest-adopted software in history: ChatGPT reached \textbf{100 million} users in
        about \textbf{two months} (early 2023).
      \item \textbf{One} general model writes essays, debugs code, tutors mathematics,
        translates, and drafts email -- no task-specific training.
      \item The jump in scale: \textbf{GPT-3} (2020, 175B params) $\rightarrow$ today's
        \textbf{GPT-5} / \textbf{Claude Opus} generation.
    \end{itemize}
    \column{0.25\textwidth}

    Under the hood this is still a neural network predicting the next token -- the same
    ``stack of affine maps + nonlinearities'' idea, scaled up enormously and trained on much
    of the public internet.}}

\begin{frame}{Generative media: text $\rightarrow$ image, audio, video}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.6\textwidth}

      \item Type a sentence, get a photorealistic \textbf{image} (Midjourney, Stable
        Diffusion) -- or now a \textbf{video}.
      \item 2025--26 video models (\textbf{Veo 3}, \textbf{Sora 2}) produce 8--20s clips with
        \textbf{synchronized audio}, consistent characters, and plausible physics --
        ``the \emph{GPT-3.5 moment} for video''.
      \item The same family of models clones voices and generates music.
    \end{itemize}

\begin{frame}{From chatbots to agents}
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item The newest systems don't just \emph{answer} -- they \textbf{act}: browse the web,
      run code, call external tools, and complete multi-step tasks on their own.
    \item \textbf{Coding agents} write and edit whole codebases; \textbf{``computer-use''}
      models click through real apps; \textbf{``deep-research''} agents read many sources and
      write a report.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.96\linewidth}{\small
    The model shifts from an \emph{oracle} (you ask, it answers) to a \emph{worker} (it does the
    task). This is exactly why ``tool use'' and ``agents'' are now core research topics --
    and, in fact, how the slides in front of you were assembled.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Embodied AI: robots that learn}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.66\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item The hard part of robotics was always \textbf{control} -- hand-writing rules for
        balance and manipulation.
      \item \textbf{Figure's Helix} (2026): a single \textbf{10-million-parameter} neural
        network replaced roughly \textbf{109{,}000 lines} of hand-tuned C++ balance code.
      \item Humanoids (\textbf{Tesla Optimus}, \textbf{Figure}) now do real factory tasks;
        NVIDIA calls this wave \textbf{``physical AI''}.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      Same recipe as vision and language: \textbf{replace hand-engineered rules with a network
      trained on data.} Representation learning, now for motor control.}}
    \column{0.32\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=5.0cm]{img/optimus.png}\\[2pt]
    {\scriptsize Tesla Optimus (2025)}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{AI for science I -- biology's 50-year problem}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.6\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item Predicting a protein's \textbf{3D shape} from its amino-acid sequence stumped
        biology for about \textbf{50 years}.
      \item \textbf{AlphaFold2} (DeepMind, 2020) cracked it, and has since predicted
        \textbf{200M+ structures} -- essentially every protein known to science -- used by
        \textbf{2M+ researchers} in 190 countries.
      \item \textbf{2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry}: Hassabis \& Jumper (shared with David
        Baker for protein \emph{design}).
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 2pt
    \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
      The \textbf{first Nobel} for a deep-learning breakthrough -- it directly speeds up drug
      and vaccine design.}}
    \column{0.4\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{img/protein.png}\\[2pt]
    {\scriptsize A protein's 3D structure\\ (ribbon model, hemoglobin)}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{AI for science II -- mathematics}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.73\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{IMO 2025} (Int'l Mathematical Olympiad): Google DeepMind
        (\emph{Gemini Deep Think}) and OpenAI both reached \textbf{gold-medal level} --
        \textbf{35/42}, solving 5 of 6 problems, writing full proofs \textbf{in natural
        language} within the 4.5h limit. (In 2024 the same feat needed problems
        hand-translated into Lean and days of compute.)
      \item \textbf{Jan 2026:} \textbf{GPT-5.2} produced a proof \emph{disproving} a conjecture
        (an infinite family of counterexamples), formalized in \textbf{Lean} and checked by
        Fields medalist \textbf{Terence Tao}.
    \end{itemize}
    \column{0.25\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{img/tao.png}\\[2pt]
    {\scriptsize Terence Tao\\ (Fields Medal 2006)}
  \end{columns}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armorange}{armorange!12}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    \textbf{Reality check.} These are \emph{early, uneven} results and some headlines were
    overhyped. But rigorous, \textbf{machine-verified} mathematics from a general model is
    genuinely new.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{AI for science III -- the physical world}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.66\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{Weather:} \textbf{GraphCast} makes 10-day forecasts faster \emph{and} more
        accurate than the gold-standard physics simulator -- it called Hurricane Lee's
        landfall \textbf{3 days} ahead of traditional models.
      \item \textbf{Materials:} \textbf{GNoME} predicted \textbf{2.2 million} new stable
        crystals -- roughly \textbf{800 years} of discovery at once.
      \item \textbf{Energy:} DeepMind used reinforcement learning to \textbf{control the
        plasma} inside a fusion reactor (now partnered with Commonwealth Fusion).
      \item \textbf{Games (the spark):} \textbf{AlphaGo} beat the world's \#1 Go player in
        2016--17 -- long thought a decade away.
    \end{itemize}
    \column{0.32\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{img/hurricane.png}\\[2pt]
    {\scriptsize Hurricane Lee (2023) --\\ GraphCast called its track early}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{AI for science IV -- it sharpens its own tools}
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{AlphaTensor} (DeepMind, 2022) discovered a \textbf{faster way to multiply
      matrices} -- the first improvement on Strassen's method in \textbf{50 years}.
    \item \textbf{AlphaDev} (2023) found \textbf{faster sorting routines}, now shipping in the
      \textbf{C++ standard library} -- run billions of times a day on real devices.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armred}{armred!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    The operation AlphaTensor sped up -- \textbf{matrix multiplication} -- is exactly what every
    neural network runs on (each layer is one big matrix multiply). AI is now optimizing
    \textbf{the very math it runs on}.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Where this chapter goes next}
  All of the above are \textbf{applications}. The rest of the chapter builds the
  \textbf{machinery} that makes them possible:
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{The single neuron} -- a weighted sum plus a nonlinearity (you already know it
      as logistic regression).
    \item \textbf{Hidden layers} -- stacking neurons so the model learns its own features
      (representation learning).
    \item \textbf{Training} -- backpropagation and gradient descent, the algorithm that fits
      the millions of weights.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    Everything in the gallery is the same core idea -- \textbf{a stack of affine maps and
    nonlinearities, trained on data} -- scaled up and specialized. We start from one neuron.}}
\end{frame}
