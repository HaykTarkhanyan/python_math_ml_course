% HOOK
\begin{frame}{Great at examples, clumsy at instructions}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.58\textwidth}
    A large LM like GPT-3 is a strong \textbf{few-shot} learner: show it a couple of
    examples and it plays along. But hand it a bare \textbf{zero-shot} instruction -- ``Does
    this premise entail this hypothesis?'' -- and it often flubs it.
    \vskip 3pt
    Why? Next-token pretraining never rewarded ``\emph{do what this instruction says}.'' The
    model saw text, not tasks. (Same framing as InstructGPT, LLM-10.)
    \vskip 3pt
    \textbf{FLAN's question:} can we make a model good at \textbf{brand-new tasks with zero
    examples}, just by teaching it the \emph{habit} of following instructions?
    \vskip 3pt
    {\footnotesize Matched pair to watch: \textbf{LIMA (LLM-4)} answers ``curate 1{,}000
    examples''; FLAN answers ``\textbf{scale up} the number of tasks.''}
    \column{0.40\textwidth}
    \begin{center}

        \node[draw=gray, thick, rounded corners=3pt, fill=gray!8,

\section{The idea: teach the habit, not the tasks}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\color{popblue}\Large\bfseries Teach the habit, not the tasks}\\[4pt]
    \small You are not teaching 60 tasks. You are teaching one skill: read an instruction, do what it asks.
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Intuition: the new hire who learns to take a ticket}
  Picture a support agent on their first week. They handle password resets, refund requests,
  shipping questions -- \textbf{60 kinds of ticket}. Then a \textbf{61st kind} they have never
  seen lands in the queue, and they handle it fine.
  \vskip 8pt
  Why? They did not memorize 60 scripts. They learned the \textbf{format of the job}:
  \emph{read the request, figure out what is being asked, produce the answer.}
  \vskip 8pt
  \keybox{FLAN's bet in one line: finetune on enough tasks phrased as instructions, and the
    model stops learning \emph{those tasks} and starts learning \textbf{``follow an
    instruction''} -- a skill that transfers to instructions it never trained on.}
  \vskip 6pt
  {\footnotesize This is the opposite failure mode from overfitting: the win is
  \emph{generalization to new task types}, not memorizing the training tasks.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Instruction tuning, defined}
  \textbf{Instruction tuning} sits between pretraining and use: a round of \textbf{multitask
  finetuning} where every example is reformatted as
  \[
    (\text{instruction},\ \text{input}) \ \longrightarrow\ \text{output}.
  \]
  \begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.68\textwidth]{fig/instruction_tuning.pdf}
  \end{center}
  \vskip -4pt
  \keybox{\footnotesize It borrows from both worlds: the \textbf{supervision} of
    pretrain--finetune and the \textbf{instruction interface} of prompting. The payoff is
    measured on a \textbf{held-out cluster} the model was never tuned on.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{One task, two framings}
  Take an NLI example. Instruction tuning is just \textbf{rewriting} it as a natural-language
  instruction -- then training on many such rewrites.
  \vskip 4pt
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \begin{center}\footnotesize\textbf{\color{armred}Raw dataset row}\end{center}
    \fcolorbox{armred}{armred!6}{\parbox{0.93\linewidth}{\footnotesize
      \texttt{premise:} a dog runs in the park\\
      \texttt{hypothesis:} an animal moves\\
      \texttt{label:} entailment}}
    \vskip 3pt
    {\footnotesize The model just learns this \emph{schema} -- it never learns to \emph{obey}
    a request.}
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \begin{center}\footnotesize\textbf{\color{paramgreen}Rewritten as an instruction}\end{center}
    \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.93\linewidth}{\footnotesize
      ``A dog runs in the park.'' Using only this, is ``an animal moves'' \textbf{true},
      \textbf{false}, or \textbf{undetermined}?\\[2pt]
      $\rightarrow$ \textbf{true}}}
    \vskip 3pt
    {\footnotesize $\sim$10 templates like this per dataset, across 62 datasets.}
  \end{columns}
  \vskip 4pt
  \keybox{\footnotesize Same data, rephrased. Do this across \emph{many} task types and the
    model learns the meta-skill ``\textbf{read the instruction, do what it asks}'' -- which
    then transfers to instructions it never saw.}
\end{frame}

\section{The method}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\color{popblue}\Large\bfseries The method}\\[4pt]
    \small 62 datasets, 12 clusters, hand-written templates -- and one strict rule for ``unseen.''
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{62 datasets, 12 clusters, $\sim$10 templates each}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.46\textwidth}
    \textbf{The data recipe:}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{62} public NLP datasets, grouped into \textbf{12 task clusters} (NLI, QA,
        summarization, translation, sentiment, \dots).
      \item Each dataset gets \textbf{$\sim$10 hand-written instruction templates}; up to
        \textbf{3} ``turn the task around'' (e.g. \emph{generate} a review from its label).
      \item Base model: \textbf{137B LaMDA-PT} (dense, decoder-only, LM-only pretrained).
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 2pt
    \keybox{\footnotesize No new labels are collected. Existing datasets are re-\emph{verbalized}
      as natural-language instructions.}
    \column{0.54\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{fig/task_clusters.pdf}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The rigorous eval: hold out the whole cluster}
  How do you prove ``zero-shot on an unseen task'' when you finetuned on 60 tasks? FLAN uses a
  \textbf{conservative} definition of unseen.
  \vskip 6pt
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item To score cluster $c$ (say NLI), \textbf{drop every dataset in $c$'s cluster} from
      finetuning, tune on the rest, then evaluate on $c$.
    \item So ``unseen NLI'' means \emph{no} NLI dataset -- not just a different NLI dataset --
      was ever seen. Each evaluated cluster uses its \textbf{own} checkpoint.
  \end{itemize}
  \vskip 4pt
  \keybox{This held-out-\textbf{cluster} design (stricter than held-out-dataset) is what makes
    the zero-shot claim honest: the model truly never met that task type.}
  \vskip 4pt
  \watchbox{\footnotesize Cost of the honesty: you train a \emph{separate} model per held-out
    cluster. The number in a results table is really an average over several tuned models.}
\end{frame}

\section{Results}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\color{popblue}\Large\bfseries Does it work?}\\[4pt]
    \small Zero-shot FLAN vs a 175B model that is 1.3x larger.
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{FLAN 137B beats GPT-3 175B -- zero-shot, and often few-shot}
  \begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig/flan_vs_gpt3.pdf}
  \end{center}
  \vskip -4pt
  Zero-shot FLAN outperforms \textbf{zero-shot} GPT-3 on \textbf{20 of 25 datasets}, and beats
  \textbf{few-shot} GPT-3 on \textbf{10} -- by a large margin on ANLI, RTE, BoolQ, AI2-ARC,
  OpenbookQA, and StoryCloze. All from a \emph{smaller} base model.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The honest caveat: it does not help everything}
  Instruction tuning does \textbf{not} help tasks that are \emph{already} phrased like the LM
  objective. Commonsense reasoning and coreference posed as \textbf{sentence completions}
  (``finish this sentence'') are basically next-token prediction already.
  \vskip 6pt
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item On \textbf{7} such commonsense / coreference tasks, FLAN beat the untuned LaMDA-PT
      on only \textbf{3 of 7}.
    \item There the instruction is \textbf{redundant} -- it adds nothing the model was not
      already doing, so there is no habit to gain.
  \end{itemize}
  \vskip 4pt
  \keybox{This sharpens the thesis: the win comes from the \textbf{instructions}, not from
    ``more data.'' Where the instruction carries real information (NLI, QA, translation), FLAN
    surges; where it is redundant, it does not.}
\end{frame}

\section{What makes it work: the ablations}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\color{popblue}\Large\bfseries What actually makes it work}\\[4pt]
    \small Three knobs: number of clusters, model scale, and the instructions themselves.
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Knob 1: more task clusters $\to$ better generalization}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.56\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig/num_clusters.pdf}
    \column{0.44\textwidth}
    Hold out NLI, closed-book QA, and commonsense; add the other clusters to finetuning
    \textbf{one at a time}.
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item Held-out zero-shot accuracy \textbf{keeps climbing} with each cluster added.
      \item At 7 clusters it \textbf{still has not saturated} -- more diversity would likely
        help further.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    \keybox{\footnotesize The driver is instruction \textbf{diversity}: many \emph{kinds} of
      task teach ``follow instructions'' better than more data of one kind.}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Knob 2: predict first -- does this help a \emph{small} model?}
  We tune models of \textbf{422M, 2B, 8B, 68B, 137B} the same way. Instruction tuning clearly
  helps the 137B. \textbf{Before the next slide: what happens to the 422M model?}
  \vskip 6pt
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item[(a)] Helps, just less -- smaller model, smaller boost.
    \item[(b)] No effect -- too small to notice.
    \item[(c)] It \textbf{hurts} -- the small model gets \emph{worse} at unseen tasks.
  \end{itemize}
  \vskip 4pt
  \pause
  \nextbox{\footnotesize Answer: \textbf{(c)}. Below a threshold, instruction tuning
    \textbf{hurts} zero-shot generalization. The benefit is an \textbf{emergent ability of
    scale} -- the same shape as chain-of-thought's emergence (LLM-14).}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Knob 2: instruction tuning is emergent with scale}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.58\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig/scale_emergence.pdf}
    \column{0.42\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{68B and 137B:} instruction tuning \textbf{helps} -- big gains on held-out
        tasks.
      \item \textbf{8B and below:} it \textbf{hurts} -- the tuned model does \emph{worse} than
        the untuned one.
      \item The \textbf{crossover is between 8B and 68B}.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    \keybox{\footnotesize Likely why: a small model spends its \emph{whole} capacity just
      fitting the $\sim$40 finetuning tasks, leaving nothing for a transferable ``follow
      instructions'' skill. A big model fits them \emph{and} has room to spare.}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Knob 3: are the instructions actually necessary?}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.56\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig/instructions_ablation.pdf}
    \column{0.44\textwidth}
    Maybe the gain is just \textbf{multitask exposure} and the instructions are decoration?
    Test it: finetune on the same datasets but \textbf{strip the templates}.
    \begin{itemize}\footnotesize
      \item \textbf{FLAN} (real instructions): \textbf{55.2}
      \item Dataset-name tag only: \textbf{46.6 / 47.0}
      \item \textbf{No template} (plain input$\to$output): \textbf{37.3}
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    \warnbox{\footnotesize Remove the instructions and the gain \textbf{collapses}. It is the
      \emph{instructions}, not merely multitask finetuning, that teaches the skill.}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\section{Recap}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\color{popblue}\Large\bfseries Wrapping up}\\[4pt]
    \small Two theses about SFT data, and where FLAN sits in the post-training story.
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{FLAN vs LIMA: the matched pair}
  Both take a pretrained LM and do \textbf{supervised finetuning} to make it usable. They
  disagree on \emph{what data}.
  \vskip 4pt
  \begin{center}
  \small
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
  \begin{tabular}{l l l}
    \toprule
     & \textbf{FLAN (LLM-18)} & \textbf{LIMA (LLM-4)}\\
    \midrule
    Thesis & scale up the \emph{tasks} & curate a few \emph{examples}\\
    Data & 62 datasets, 12 clusters & 1{,}000 hand-picked prompts\\
    Goal & zero-shot on \textbf{unseen task types} & helpful, well-formatted answers\\
    Bet & instruction \textbf{diversity} & example \textbf{quality}\\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \vskip 6pt
  \keybox{\footnotesize Not a contradiction: FLAN maximizes \emph{task coverage} for
    cross-task generalization; LIMA argues the \emph{format/quality} of a small set is enough
    once the base model is strong. Different questions, different answers.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Recap}
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{Instruction tuning} = multitask finetuning where every example is phrased as
      an instruction: $(\text{instruction}, \text{input})\to\text{output}$. It teaches a
      transferable \textbf{``follow instructions''} skill.
    \item \textbf{Setup}: 137B LaMDA-PT, \textbf{62 datasets / 12 clusters}, $\sim$10 templates
      each; ``unseen'' means the \textbf{whole cluster} is held out.
    \item \textbf{Result}: zero-shot FLAN $>$ zero-shot GPT-3 175B on \textbf{20/25 datasets},
      $>$ few-shot GPT-3 on \textbf{10}. But it does \emph{not} help tasks already close to the
      LM objective (\textbf{3 of 7} commonsense/coreference).
    \item \textbf{What matters}: many clusters (diversity), \textbf{scale} (helps $\geq$68B,
      \textbf{hurts} $\leq$8B), and the \textbf{instructions themselves} (strip them, gain
      collapses: 55.2 $\to$ 37.3).
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \nextbox{\textbf{Next:} \emph{[LLM-4] LIMA} is the deliberate counterpoint -- quality over
    quantity. And FLAN's ``instructions as SFT data'' is the engine behind step 1 of
    \emph{[LLM-10] InstructGPT} and modern post-training. (Follow-up, do not conflate:
    \emph{FLAN-T5} / ``Scaling Instruction-Finetuned LMs,'' 2022 -- same idea, bigger and
    with CoT data.)}
\end{frame}
