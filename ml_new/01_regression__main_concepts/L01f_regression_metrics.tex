\begin{frame}{Loss vs.\ metric: what's the difference?}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Loss.} The function the model \emph{optimizes during fitting}. Must be differentiable for gradient-based methods.
    \vskip 4pt
    \emph{Examples:} MSE for OLS, log loss for logistic regression.
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Metric.} The number you \emph{report} to humans. Doesn't have to be differentiable.
    \vskip 4pt
    \emph{Examples:} RMSE in kAMD, MAE, MAPE, $R^2$.
  \end{columns}
  \vfill
  Loss and metric \emph{can} be the same (often are for regression) - but they don't have to be. You might train to minimize MSE because of its math properties, then report MAE because stakeholders prefer ``typical error in real units.''
  \vfill
  \textbf{The key question:} \emph{what kind of mistake hurts you most?} The right metric depends on the answer.
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item One big error vs.\ many small ones?
    \item Errors on cheap items vs.\ expensive items?
    \item Over- vs.\ under-prediction?
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Magnitude metrics}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{MSE, RMSE, MAE: how the penalty shapes differ}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \[
      \text{MSE} = \tfrac{1}{n} \sum (y_i - \yh_i)^2
      \quad\;\;
      \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\text{MSE}}
    \]
    \[
      \text{MAE} = \tfrac{1}{n} \sum |y_i - \yh_i|
    \]
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{MSE/RMSE} penalize residuals \emph{quadratically}: one error of 100 contributes $10\,000$; ten errors of 10 contribute $1000$ total. Big errors dominate.
      \item \textbf{MAE} penalizes \emph{linearly}: one big and ten small contribute the same total. Robust.
      \item Report RMSE to humans (units of $y$). Use MSE internally (smoother for optimization).
    \end{itemize}
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \begin{tikzpicture}
        \begin{axis}[width=\textwidth, height=4cm,
                     xlabel={residual $e = y - \hat{y}$}, ylabel={penalty},
                     xlabel style={font=\scriptsize}, ylabel style={font=\scriptsize},
                     xtick=\empty, ytick=\empty, axis line style={gray!60},
                     legend pos=north west, legend style={font=\scriptsize},
                     xmin=-5, xmax=5, ymin=-1, ymax=12]
          \addplot[armred, very thick, domain=-5:5, samples=100] {x*x};
          \addlegendentry{$e^2$ (MSE)}
          \addplot[armblue, very thick, domain=-5:5, samples=2] {abs(x)};
          \addlegendentry{$|e|$ (MAE)}
        \end{axis}
      \end{tikzpicture}\\[-2pt]
      \begin{tikzpicture}
        \begin{axis}[width=\textwidth, height=2.5cm,
                     xlabel style={font=\tiny}, ylabel style={font=\tiny},
                     xtick=\empty, ytick=\empty, axis line style={gray!60},
                     xmin=0, xmax=6, ymin=0, ymax=7,
                     title={data + residuals}, title style={font=\scriptsize}]
          \addplot[armblue, very thick, domain=0.3:5.7, samples=2] {0.9*x + 1};

\begin{frame}[fragile]{MAE and MedAE: robust variants}
  \[
    \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n |y_i - \yh_i|
    \qquad
    \text{MedAE} = \text{median}_i\, |y_i - \yh_i|
  \]
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MAE.} Same units as $y$. Penalizes errors \emph{linearly} - an error of 100 contributes 100, an error of 10 contributes 10. Outliers don't dominate.
    \item \textbf{MedAE.} The median absolute residual. Even more robust than MAE - half the errors are at most this big.
    \item Where each shines: \emph{MAE} for real-estate, finance, anything with outlier-heavy targets. \emph{MedAE} for ``typical case'' reporting (``half of my predictions are within 30 kAMD'').
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
{\lstset{basicstyle=\ttfamily\scriptsize}
\begin{lstlisting}
from sklearn.metrics import mean_absolute_error, median_absolute_error
mae    = mean_absolute_error(y, y_pred)
medae  = median_absolute_error(y, y_pred)
\end{lstlisting}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Predict-first: which metric notices the outlier?}
  10 predictions of Yerevan rent. 9 are off by 1 kAMD, 1 is off by 100 kAMD (a luxury apartment your model missed).
  \vfill
  \pause
  \textbf{Predict the four metrics:}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item MSE? \quad RMSE? \quad MAE? \quad MedAE?
  \end{itemize}
  \pause
  \vfill
  \begin{center}\small
    \begin{tabular}{lcl}
      Metric & Value & Interpretation \\ \hline
      MSE   & $\dfrac{9 \cdot 1^2 + 1 \cdot 100^2}{10} = 1000.9$ & dominated by the outlier \\[4pt]
      RMSE  & $\sqrt{1000.9} \approx 31.6$ & ``typical error 32 kAMD'' (misleading) \\[2pt]
      MAE   & $\dfrac{9 \cdot 1 + 100}{10} = 10.9$ & visible but moderate \\[4pt]
      MedAE & $\text{median} = 1$ & outlier completely invisible \\
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
  \footnotesize \textbf{Lesson:} same model, four different stories. The metric you pick \emph{is} the conclusion you'll report.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{RMSLE: for skewed positive targets}
  \[
    \text{RMSLE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \bigl( \log(1+y_i) - \log(1+\yh_i) \bigr)^2 }
  \]
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Penalizes \emph{relative} errors, not absolute. Predicting 100 when truth is 110 has the same RMSLE as predicting 1000 when truth is 1100 (both $\sim 10\%$ off).
    \item Penalizes under-prediction harder than over-prediction (the log function bends that way).
    \item Default for prices, sales, counts - anything strictly positive and skewed. Kaggle's go-to for housing-price and demand-forecasting competitions.
    \item Pairs with the \texttt{log1p} transform from L01c.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
{\lstset{basicstyle=\ttfamily\scriptsize}
\begin{lstlisting}
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_log_error
import numpy as np
rmsle = np.sqrt(mean_squared_log_error(y, y_pred))
\end{lstlisting}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Magnitude metrics: comparison cheat sheet}
  \begin{center}\small
    \begin{tabular}{lllll}
      Metric & Units & Outlier penalty & Best for & Watch out for \\ \hline
      MSE   & $y^2$       & quadratic       & training loss            & not interpretable \\
      RMSE  & $y$         & quadratic       & reporting in real units  & inflated by outliers \\
      MAE   & $y$         & linear          & robust point estimate    & ignores error direction \\
      MedAE & $y$         & zero            & ``typical'' error        & ignores half the data \\
      RMSLE & log-units   & relative        & skewed positive targets  & undefined for $y < 0$ \\
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
  Lower is better for all five. None of them can be compared across datasets with different $y$ scales (Yerevan rent in kAMD vs.\ Berlin rent in EUR).
  \vfill
  \footnotesize \emph{Two-line rule of thumb:} report \textbf{RMSE} if outliers matter to you, \textbf{MAE} if they don't, \textbf{RMSLE} if the target is multiplicative.
\end{frame}

\section{Relative metrics}

\begin{frame}{$R^2$ and adjusted $R^2$}
  \[
    R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum (y_i - \yh_i)^2}{\sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2}
    \qquad
    R^2_\text{adj} = 1 - (1 - R^2)\, \frac{n-1}{n-p-1}
  \]
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{$R^2$}: fraction of variance explained, relative to the constant-mean baseline. $R^2 = 1$ is perfect; $R^2 = 0$ means ``no better than predicting $\bar{y}$''.
    \item \textbf{Adjusted $R^2$} penalizes adding features that don't help. $R^2$ \emph{always} increases when you add a feature; adjusted $R^2$ can decrease.
    \item Scale-free $\Rightarrow$ comparable across datasets (unlike MAE/RMSE).
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \textbf{Generalized $R^2$.} The same skeleton works with any loss and any baseline:
  \[
    R^2_\text{gen} \;=\; 1 - \frac{\text{Loss}_\text{complex}}{\text{Loss}_\text{baseline}}
  \]
  Classical $R^2$ is the special case (loss $=$ SSE, baseline $=$ constant mean). Use any loss, any reference. On test data, when the complex model is worse than the baseline, this is negative.
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armred}{armred!8}{\parbox{0.96\textwidth}{\small
    \textbf{$R^2$ on test can be negative.} It just means your model is worse than predicting the training mean. Investigate.
  }}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Better $R^2 \neq$ better fit}
  Same model, same MSE, two different views of the data. R$^2$ depends on the \emph{range} of $y$; MSE depends on the \emph{magnitude} of residuals.
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}
      \begin{groupplot}[
        group style={group size=2 by 1, horizontal sep=20pt},
        width=0.42\textwidth, height=4cm,
        xmin=0, xmax=1, ymin=-0.6, ymax=1.5,
        xtick=\empty, ytick=\empty, axis line style={gray!60},
        title style={font=\footnotesize},
      ]
        \nextgroupplot[title={half range: $R^2 = 0.74$, MSE $= 0.02$}]
          \addplot[only marks, mark=*, mark size=1.2pt, armred] coordinates {
            (0.05, 0.12) (0.12, 0.08) (0.18, 0.30) (0.25, 0.16) (0.32, 0.42)
            (0.40, 0.34) (0.45, 0.58) (0.48, 0.46)};
          \addplot[armblue, very thick, domain=0:0.55, samples=2] {1.1*x + 0.03};
        \nextgroupplot[title={full range: $R^2 = 0.83$, MSE $= 0.02$}]
          \addplot[only marks, mark=*, mark size=1.2pt, armred] coordinates {
            (0.05, 0.12) (0.12, 0.08) (0.18, 0.30) (0.25, 0.16) (0.32, 0.42)
            (0.40, 0.34) (0.45, 0.58) (0.48, 0.46) (0.58, 0.55) (0.65, 0.85)
            (0.72, 0.72) (0.80, 0.95) (0.88, 1.05) (0.95, 1.18)};
          \addplot[armblue, very thick, domain=0:1, samples=2] {1.1*x + 0.03};
      \end{groupplot}
    \end{tikzpicture}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
  \footnotesize \textbf{Why?} $R^2 = 1 - \text{SSE}/\text{SST}$. Extending the range inflates SST much faster than SSE $\Rightarrow$ ratio shrinks $\Rightarrow$ $R^2$ goes up - even though the fit didn't actually improve.
  \vskip 2pt
  \textbf{Lesson:} $R^2$ is invariant under linear rescaling of $y$ in a way MSE isn't. Don't compare $R^2$ across datasets with different $y$-ranges. Pair it with RMSE/MAE.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{MAPE: the popular but flawed default}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.55\textwidth}
    \[
      \text{MAPE} = \frac{100\%}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{|y_i - \yh_i|}{|y_i|}
    \]
    Popular because it reports as a percentage. Stakeholders love it.
    \vskip 4pt
    \footnotesize Three known problems:
    \begin{enumerate}\footnotesize
      \item \textbf{Undefined when $y_i = 0$.} Any zero target $\Rightarrow$ division by zero $\Rightarrow$ infinite MAPE.
      \item \textbf{Asymmetric.} Under-prediction capped at 100\%; over-prediction unbounded. Plot shows this for fixed truth $y=10$.
      \item \textbf{Biases models toward under-prediction} when used as a loss.
    \end{enumerate}
    \column{0.45\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \begin{tikzpicture}

                     xlabel={prediction $\hat{y}$ (truth $=10$)},

\begin{frame}[fragile]{SMAPE: the symmetric fix}
  \[
    \text{SMAPE} = \frac{100\%}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{2\,|y_i - \yh_i|}{|y_i| + |\yh_i|}
  \]
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item Symmetric: a 50\% over-prediction and a 50\% under-prediction get the same score.
    \item Bounded in $[0\%, 200\%]$ - no infinities.
    \item Defined when $y_i = 0$ (as long as $\yh_i \neq 0$).
    \item Still has its own quirks (the bound of 200\% means a missed prediction of 0 dominates), but generally better-behaved than MAPE.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
{\lstset{basicstyle=\ttfamily\scriptsize}
\begin{lstlisting}
import numpy as np
def smape(y, y_pred):
    return 200 * np.mean(np.abs(y - y_pred) / (np.abs(y) + np.abs(y_pred)))
\end{lstlisting}}
  \vfill
  \footnotesize \textbf{MASE} (Mean Absolute Scaled Error): MAE of the model divided by MAE of a naïve forecast (e.g.,\ ``predict last value''). Scale-free, robust to zeros, standard for time-series competitions (M4, M5).
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{MBE: catching systematic bias}
  \[
    \text{MBE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (\yh_i - y_i)
  \]
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Positive} $\Rightarrow$ model systematically over-predicts.
    \item \textbf{Negative} $\Rightarrow$ model systematically under-predicts.
    \item Symmetric error metrics (MSE, MAE) \emph{hide} bias - $+50$ and $-50$ errors cancel in MBE but add in MAE.
    \item Critical for forecasting and demand planning, where a $1\%$ persistent bias compounds across millions of items.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \textbf{Rule:} report MBE alongside MAE / RMSE whenever the cost of over-prediction differs from the cost of under-prediction.
  \vfill
  \footnotesize \emph{Yerevan example:} a rent-estimator with $\text{MBE} = +25$ kAMD over-prices every listing. Landlords love it; the marketplace loses to competitors with honest estimators.
\end{frame}

  \vskip -2pt
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.55\textwidth}
    \footnotesize
    \textbf{Pearson $r$} - linear co-movement: $r = \text{cov}(y,\yh)/(\sigma_y\sigma_{\yh})$. High when relationship is straight.
    \vskip 2pt
    \textbf{Spearman $\rho$} - Pearson on \emph{ranks}. Robust to monotonic transforms; doesn't need linearity.
    \vskip 2pt
    \textbf{Kendall $\tau$} - fraction of concordant pairs minus discordant. Robust to ties, slower to compute.
    \column{0.45\textwidth}
{\lstset{basicstyle=\ttfamily\scriptsize}
\begin{lstlisting}
from scipy.stats import (
  pearsonr, spearmanr, kendalltau)
r, _   = pearsonr(y, y_pred)
rho, _ = spearmanr(y, y_pred)
tau, _ = kendalltau(y, y_pred)
\end{lstlisting}}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Predict-first: when correlation lies}
  Your model's predictions on a 3-row test set:
  \vskip 4pt
  \begin{center}\small
    \begin{tabular}{lccc}
      $y_\text{true}$ & 100 & 200 & 300 \\
      $\yh$           & 1100 & 1200 & 1300 \\
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
  \pause
  \textbf{Predict:}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Pearson correlation $r$ between $y$ and $\yh$?
    \item MAE?
    \item Would you deploy this model?
  \end{itemize}
  \pause
  \vfill
  Answers:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pearson $r = 1.0$.} The predictions are perfectly linearly related to truth - they're just shifted by $+1000$.
    \item \textbf{MAE $= 1000$.} The predictions are uniformly terrible.
    \item No.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armred}{armred!8}{\parbox{0.96\textwidth}{\small
    \textbf{Correlation measures association, not accuracy.} Use correlations for screening features. Use MAE/RMSE for evaluating models.
  }}
\end{frame}

\section{Information criteria (AIC, BIC)}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{AIC and BIC: model comparison, not evaluation}
  \[
    \text{AIC} = 2k - 2 \ln(\hat{L})
    \qquad
    \text{BIC} = k \ln(n) - 2 \ln(\hat{L})
  \]
  where $k$ = number of model parameters, $n$ = sample size, $\hat{L}$ = maximized likelihood.
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Both compare \emph{candidate models on the same data}. Lower is better.
    \item They balance \textbf{fit quality} ($-2 \ln \hat{L}$) against \textbf{model complexity} ($k$).
    \item Require a probabilistic model so $\hat{L}$ is defined - typical with \texttt{statsmodels} OLS, not directly with sklearn estimators.
    \item These are \emph{model selection} criteria, not accuracy metrics. They tell you which model to pick; they do not tell you how accurate that model is on unseen data.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
{\lstset{basicstyle=\ttfamily\scriptsize}
\begin{lstlisting}
import statsmodels.api as sm
res = sm.OLS(y, sm.add_constant(X)).fit()
print(res.aic, res.bic)
\end{lstlisting}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{AIC vs.\ BIC: when each wins}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.55\textwidth}
    Both penalize complexity, but \textbf{BIC penalizes harder} once $n \geq 8$ (because $\ln n > 2$).
    \vskip 6pt
    \textbf{AIC} ($2k$ per param):
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item Better for \emph{prediction} - keeps useful features.
      \item Asymptotically equivalent to leave-one-out CV.
    \end{itemize}
    \textbf{BIC} ($\ln(n)\cdot k$ per param):
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item Better for \emph{model identification} - conservative.
      \item Consistent: as $n \to \infty$, BIC picks the true model.
    \end{itemize}
    \textbf{Practical:} report both. When they disagree, BIC is more conservative. Pair with CV for honest accuracy.
    \column{0.45\textwidth}
    \begin{center}
      \begin{tikzpicture}
        \begin{axis}[width=\textwidth, height=4.5cm,
                     xlabel={sample size $n$ (log scale)},
                     ylabel={penalty per parameter},
                     xlabel style={font=\scriptsize}, ylabel style={font=\scriptsize},
                     axis line style={gray!60},
                     xmin=2, xmax=1000, ymin=0, ymax=8,
                     xmode=log,
                     xtick={2, 8, 100, 1000},
                     xticklabel style={font=\tiny},

                     grid=major, grid style={dashed,gray!30},
                     legend pos=north west, legend style={font=\scriptsize}]
          \addplot[armred, very thick, domain=2:1000, samples=2] {2};
          \addlegendentry{AIC ($=2$)}
          \addplot[armblue, very thick, domain=2:1000, samples=100] {ln(x)};
          \addlegendentry{BIC ($=\ln n$)}
          \draw[gray!60, dashed] (axis cs:7.4, 0) -- (axis cs:7.4, 8);
          \node[gray, font=\tiny, anchor=south] at (axis cs:7.4, 0) {$n{=}e^2$};
        \end{axis}
      \end{tikzpicture}\\
      \scriptsize They cross at $n = e^2 \approx 7.4$. Beyond that, BIC's complexity tax is harsher.
    \end{center}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\section{Residual diagnostics}

\begin{frame}{The four standard diagnostic plots}
  Numerical metrics give you \emph{one number}. Plots show you \emph{what kind of wrong} your model is. Four standard plots (the R \texttt{plot.lm} set):
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Residuals vs.\ fitted.} Are residuals patterned? (linearity, heteroscedasticity)
    \item \textbf{Scale-Location.} Does residual spread depend on prediction? (heteroscedasticity, refined)
    \item \textbf{Normal QQ.} Are residuals roughly Gaussian? (matters for OLS inference)
    \item \textbf{Residuals vs.\ leverage.} Which observations have unusual influence?
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  Look at all four. A model with perfect $R^2 = 0.97$ can still be broken in ways the diagnostic plots reveal.
  \vfill
  \texttt{statsmodels} produces these via \texttt{sm.graphics.plot\_regress\_exog} or by manual matplotlib.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Leverage and Cook's distance}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.55\textwidth}
    \footnotesize
    \textbf{Leverage $h_i$.} How unusual is $\xv^{(i)}$? Diagonal of the hat matrix $\Xmat(\Xmat^\top\Xmat)^{-1}\Xmat^\top$. High leverage $\Rightarrow$ this point can pull the fit strongly toward itself.
    \vskip 4pt
    \textbf{Cook's distance $D_i$.} How much would $\thetah$ change if you removed observation $i$? Combines leverage AND residual size.
    \[
      D_i \;=\; \frac{r_i^2}{p \cdot \text{MSE}} \cdot \frac{h_i}{(1 - h_i)^2}
    \]

\section{Prediction intervals and coverage}

\begin{frame}{Point predictions aren't enough}
  ``This apartment will rent for 310 kAMD'' is a \emph{point prediction}. It says nothing about uncertainty.
  \vfill
  ``90\% prediction interval: $[265, 355]$ kAMD'' is \textbf{honest} - it communicates what the model knows and doesn't know.
  \vfill

  \vfill
  \footnotesize Out of 8 truths, all 8 fell within the 90\% PI - actual coverage $100\%$. With a larger test set we'd expect roughly $90\%$.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{Coverage: testing a prediction interval}
  \textbf{Empirical coverage} = fraction of true values that fell inside their predicted interval.
  \vfill
  For a well-calibrated $1-\alpha$ PI, coverage on a held-out test set should be approximately $1-\alpha$. If you build 90\% PIs and only 75\% of test points land inside, your intervals are too narrow - you're claiming more confidence than you have.
  \vfill
{\lstset{basicstyle=\ttfamily\scriptsize}
\begin{lstlisting}
# Quick way: residual quantiles on a calibration split
residuals = y_cal - model.predict(X_cal)
q_lo, q_hi = np.quantile(residuals, [0.05, 0.95])     # 90% PI
y_pred = model.predict(X_test)
lo, hi = y_pred + q_lo, y_pred + q_hi
coverage = np.mean((y_test >= lo) & (y_test <= hi))
print(f"empirical coverage = {coverage:.2%}")        # should be ~90%
\end{lstlisting}}
  \vfill
  \footnotesize \textbf{Modern tool:} \texttt{MAPIE} library implements \emph{conformal prediction}, which gives provable coverage guarantees regardless of model.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Recap}
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{Magnitude metrics} (MSE, RMSE, MAE, MedAE, RMSLE) all answer ``how big are the errors?'' but differ in how they treat outliers and scale. Report at least two together.
    \item \textbf{Relative metrics} ($R^2$, adjusted $R^2$, MAPE, SMAPE, MBE) are scale-free or normalized. $R^2$ can be negative on test data; MAPE is asymmetric and ill-defined at $y=0$; MBE catches bias the symmetric metrics hide.
    \item \textbf{Correlations} measure association, not accuracy. A perfect Pearson can coexist with terrible predictions.
    \item \textbf{AIC / BIC} are for model \emph{comparison}, not absolute accuracy. Pair them with CV.
    \item \textbf{Diagnostic plots} reveal what kind of wrong your model is. Residuals vs.\ fitted, QQ, scale-location, leverage-vs-Cook's.
    \item \textbf{Prediction intervals + coverage} turn point predictions into honest uncertainty reports.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \emph{One model. Many metrics. Report the ones that match what your stakeholder will actually do with the output.}
\end{frame}
