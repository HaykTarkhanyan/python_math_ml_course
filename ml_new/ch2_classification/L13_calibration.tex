\begin{frame}{When the model says 0.9, is it bad 90\% of the time?}
  Last lecture measured how well our cheese detector \emph{ranks} batches (ROC, PR). But it
  also outputs a \textbf{number} --- $\hat p = 0.9$. If we tell the factory ``$90\%$ chance
  this batch is bad,'' is that \emph{true}?
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armred}{armred!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    A model can \textbf{rank} perfectly (high AUC) and still output probabilities that are
    systematically wrong --- saying $0.9$ when only $70\%$ of such batches are actually bad.
    That gap is \textbf{(mis)calibration}.}}
  \vfill
  This deck: what calibration is, how to measure it, and how to fix it.
\end{frame}

\section{What calibration is}

\begin{frame}{What ``calibrated'' means}
  A classifier is \textbf{calibrated} if its probabilities match reality:
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    Among all batches it scores $\hat p \approx 0.8$, about \textbf{80\%} are actually bad.}}
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}
    \item A \textbf{score} ranks; a \textbf{calibrated probability} can be read as a real chance.
    \item Calibration is about the \emph{value} of $\hat p$, not the \emph{order} --- a separate
      property from accuracy or AUC.
    \item We check it by bucketing predictions and comparing predicted vs.\ observed frequency.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The reliability diagram}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.48\textwidth}
    Bin predictions by $\hat p$; plot \textbf{predicted} ($x$) vs.\ \textbf{observed
    frequency} ($y$).
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item On the diagonal $=$ perfectly calibrated.
      \item \textbf{Below} the line $=$ \textbf{over-confident} (predicts higher than reality).
      \item Above $=$ under-confident.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    Logistic regression tracks the diagonal; naive Bayes is over-confident --- its redundant
    features double-count evidence and push $\hat p$ to the extremes (ECE $0.06$ vs.\ $0.02$).
    \column{0.52\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig/cal_reliability.pdf}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Over-confidence, seen another way}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.48\textwidth}
    Same two models, histogram of their predicted probabilities.
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{Over-confident} models pile scores near $0$ and $1$ --- they rarely say ``maybe''.
      \item Calibrated models spread mass across $[0,1]$, matching real uncertainty.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    Piling at the extremes is the fingerprint of miscalibration --- and exactly what breaks any
    decision that reads $\hat p$ as a chance.
    \column{0.52\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig/cal_hist.pdf}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\section{Why it matters}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\Large\color{popblue}\textbf{Calibration $\neq$ ranking}}\\[0.9em]
    \begin{minipage}{0.82\textwidth}\centering\small
      A model can order batches perfectly yet report wrong probabilities. AUC won't catch it
      --- and any cost decision that uses $\hat p$ will be off.
    \end{minipage}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Calibration is separate from discrimination}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Discrimination} (ROC AUC): does the score \emph{order} positives above
      negatives? Cares only about ranking.
    \item \textbf{Calibration}: are the score \emph{values} true frequencies?
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  A model can have \textbf{AUC $=1$} (perfect ranking) and still be badly miscalibrated ---
  halve every probability and the order is unchanged but the numbers are wrong.
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armred}{armred!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    \textbf{Why it matters.} The cost-optimal threshold $c^* = \frac{C_{FP}}{C_{FN}+C_{FP}}$
    and \emph{any} expected-value decision assume $\hat p$ is a true probability. Reporting
    ``$90\%$ risk'' to the factory (or a doctor) only means something if it is calibrated.}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Who is miscalibrated?}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Usually fine}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{Logistic regression} --- it minimizes log-loss, a proper scoring rule.
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 2pt
    \textbf{Over-confident} (scores too extreme)
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item Naive Bayes (independence assumption)
      \item Boosted trees, deep neural nets
      \item SVMs (margins, not probabilities)
    \end{itemize}
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Under-confident} (scores too timid)
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item Random forests (averaging pulls toward $0.5$)
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 6pt
    \fcolorbox{armorange}{armorange!12}{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\scriptsize
      \textbf{Watch out:} \texttt{class\_weight} and resampling (for imbalance) also distort
      probabilities --- they change the base rate the model trains on, so re-calibrate
      afterwards.}}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Measuring calibration}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reliability diagram} --- the picture (qualitative).
    \item \textbf{ECE} (expected calibration error): bin predictions, average
      $|\text{observed} - \text{predicted}|$ weighted by bin size. The headline number; $0$ is
      perfect. \emph{Caveat: depends on the binning.}
    \item \textbf{Brier score} $=\tfrac1n\sum(\hat p_i - y_i)^2$ --- a proper scoring rule, but
      it \emph{mixes} calibration and ranking, so a sharp model can score well while miscalibrated.
    \item \textbf{Log-loss} --- also proper; punishes confident mistakes hardest.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armblue}{armblue!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    For a \emph{pure} calibration number use \textbf{ECE} (or read the reliability diagram);
    Brier and log-loss are good overall scores but conflate calibration with discrimination.}}
\end{frame}

\section{Fixing it}

\begin{frame}[plain]
  \vfill
  \begin{center}
    {\Large\color{popblue}\textbf{Fixing calibration}}\\[0.9em]
    \begin{minipage}{0.82\textwidth}\centering\small
      Don't retrain --- learn a small post-hoc map from the model's raw scores to true
      probabilities, fit on held-out data.
    \end{minipage}
  \end{center}
  \vfill
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Post-hoc calibration: Platt and isotonic}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.48\textwidth}
    Learn a map from raw scores to true probabilities, on \textbf{held-out} data:
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item \textbf{Platt scaling}: fit a sigmoid $\sigma(a\,s + b)$. Two parameters --- good
        for \emph{small} data; assumes an S-shaped distortion.
      \item \textbf{Isotonic regression}: a free \emph{monotonic} step function. More
        flexible --- needs \emph{more} data (overfits when small).
    \end{itemize}
    \vskip 4pt
    Isotonic snaps naive Bayes onto the diagonal: ECE $0.06 \to 0.01$.
    \column{0.52\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig/cal_before_after.pdf}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{Calibration in \texttt{sklearn}}
\begin{lstlisting}
from sklearn.calibration import CalibratedClassifierCV

# wrap any classifier; calibrate by cross-validation on the training data
cal = CalibratedClassifierCV(
    GaussianNB(),
    method="isotonic",     # or "sigmoid" (Platt) for small data
    cv=5,
)
cal.fit(X_train, y_train)
cal.predict_proba(X_test)[:, 1]    # now-calibrated probabilities
\end{lstlisting}
  \vskip 2pt
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \texttt{method="sigmoid"} (Platt) for small data; \texttt{"isotonic"} for larger
      ($\gtrsim$ a few thousand).
    \item Calibrate on data the base model did \textbf{not} train on --- \texttt{cv} handles this.
    \item \texttt{CalibrationDisplay.from\_estimator(...)} draws the reliability diagram.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{In practice}

\begin{frame}{When does calibration matter?}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Calibrate when\dots}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item you \emph{report} probabilities (risk to a doctor / factory);
      \item you make \textbf{cost / expected-value} decisions or tune a threshold by cost;
      \item you \textbf{average or ensemble} probabilities from several models.
    \end{itemize}
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Don't bother when\dots}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item you only need a \textbf{ranking} (ROC AUC, top-$k$\% / lift);
      \item the threshold is tuned directly on the metric you care about.
    \end{itemize}
  \end{columns}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{armred}{armred!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    \textbf{Golden rule:} fit calibration on data the base model never saw, and don't re-tune
    the threshold on that same split --- both bias the result optimistically.}}
\end{frame}

\section*{Wrap-up}

\begin{frame}{Recap}
  \begin{itemize}\small
    \item \textbf{Calibration} $=$ predicted probabilities match observed frequencies (read it
      off the \textbf{reliability diagram}).
    \item It is \textbf{separate from ranking}: high AUC does not imply good probabilities.
    \item Measure with \textbf{ECE} (pure calibration) or Brier / log-loss (overall, proper scores).
    \item Logistic regression is usually calibrated; naive Bayes, boosted trees and nets are
      over-confident; random forests under-confident; imbalance fixes distort probabilities.
    \item Fix post-hoc with \textbf{Platt} (sigmoid, small data) or \textbf{isotonic} (flexible,
      more data) via \texttt{CalibratedClassifierCV} --- always on held-out data.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \fcolorbox{paramgreen}{paramgreen!8}{\parbox{0.97\linewidth}{\small
    \textbf{Next:} imbalanced learning --- class weights and resampling when the positive class
    is rare (and why they then need re-calibration).}}
\end{frame}
